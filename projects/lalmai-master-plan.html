01 — Overview

A national programme, built from the ground up

In 2019, the Government of Bangladesh directed the Local Government Engineering Department (LGED) to prepare master plans for every upazila in the country under the "Amar Gram, Amar Shohor" (My Village, My Town) initiative. Lalmai Upazila — a 120 sq. km, 9-union administrative area in Cumilla District formed in 2017 — is one of three upazilas Sheltech Consultants is responsible for under UTMIDP's second master-plan package (UMPP-2).

My work on this project spans two connected layers: building the geospatial infrastructure the entire plan rests on — geodatabase, topographic survey, land-use survey, facility mapping — and contributing to a custom-built Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) model that scores every parcel of land in the upazila for urban development suitability. The first is the foundation; the second is the analytical product that turns that foundation into a planning decision.

02 — GIS Database & Survey Infrastructure

From mouza maps to a centimeter-accurate spatial database

Before any analysis is possible, an upazila needs an accurate, georeferenced spatial base. That base didn't exist in usable digital form for Lalmai — it had to be built from scratch, combining legacy cadastral records with drone photogrammetry and GNSS field control.

Mouza map digitization & geodatabase construction

Legacy mouza (cadastral) maps were collected from DLRS/DC offices, scanned, and digitized with ward/union/district attributes attached. Each sheet was edge-checked on a light table and on-screen, corrected, and georeferenced through spatial adjustment before being joined into a single upazila-wide geodatabase.

Bench Mark (BM) pillar network & RTK-GNSS control

Permanent BM pillars were designed, sited against selection criteria, and constructed across the upazila, tied back to Survey of Bangladesh reference points. A baseline survey using RTK-GNSS static mode established centimeter-level coordinates for every Ground Control Point (GCP) and BM pillar, validated through network adjustment.

Drone photogrammetry & DEM/orthophoto production

A Matrice 300 RTK drone captured sub-5cm resolution aerial imagery across the full upazila under a planned flight grid with controlled overlap. The imagery was processed through aerial triangulation, dense point cloud generation, and DSM creation, producing a final orthomosaic and Digital Elevation Model used as the base layer for every subsequent survey.

Field verification & attribute collection

Physical feature, land use, and infrastructure attributes were verified and collected in the field using KoboToolbox for structured digital data capture, cross-checked against the drone-derived vector layers before being committed to the geodatabase.

04 — Urban Development Potential Index (Featured Analysis)

An AHP-weighted GIS model to decide where Lalmai should grow

Survey data describes what exists. This model answers a harder question: given everything we know about the terrain, infrastructure, and conservation constraints of this upazila, where should future urban growth actually be directed? Working alongside a team of planning colleagues, I contributed to building a replicable Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis framework — the Urban Development Potential Index (UDPI) — that converts twenty spatial factors and expert judgement into a single, defensible suitability map.

My contribution: I was a significant contributor to this model as part of the Sheltech planning team — supporting factor layer preparation, GIS weighted overlay execution, and scenario analysis, alongside a 9-member expert panel responsible for the AHP pairwise comparisons.

How the model works

The Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) was used to derive defensible weights for 20 suitability factors across 6 thematic categories, through structured pairwise comparisons by nine qualified urban planning professionals using Saaty's 1–9 scale. Every resulting comparison matrix returned a Consistency Ratio (CR) below the 0.10 acceptability threshold — confirming the panel's judgements were logically coherent, not arbitrary. Each factor was reclassified to a standardized suitability score, multiplied by its AHP-derived weight, and summed in a GIS weighted overlay to produce a continuous Urban Development Potential Index raster, classified into five suitability tiers using Jenks Natural Breaks.

05 — Plan Framework, Vision & Participatory Process

The plan that all the data feeds into

The surveys and suitability model produce inputs. The Structure Plan is the output — a legally mandated, 20-year policy framework governing how Lalmai Upazila grows, protects its environment, and delivers services to its population through to 2047.

Three-tier plan structure

Structure Plan — 20 years (2027–2047)

Long-range strategic policy for spatial growth, land use, infrastructure investment priorities, and environmental conservation at the upazila level. The AHP-UDPI model directly feeds the growth management strategy in this tier.

Urban & Rural Area Plan — 10 years

Translates structural policies into spatial plans for each of Lalmai's 9 unions — defining urban growth boundaries, rural conservation zones, facility provision standards, and detailed land-use allocations.

Action Area Plans — 5 years

Short-range, implementable plans for priority intervention zones — identifying specific development sites, infrastructure projects, and phased investment schedules tied to government budget cycles.

Plan vision

"A Liveable, Inclusive, Resilient, Innovative and Adaptive Lalmai Upazila by 2047"

Four planning pillars: Infrastructure-led growth · Inclusive service delivery · Climate resilience · Economic competitiveness — with ecosystem conservation as the plan's non-negotiable conditionality.

Participatory process

The plan rests on 58 PRA sessions and 21 Key Informant Interviews — deliberately including marginalized groups: Ashrayan residents, Hindu minorities, cobbler communities, and slum dwellers — alongside elected officials, teachers, and business owners. Dream Mapping exercises in each union produced community-owned spatial priorities that are directly reflected in the sectoral recommendations below.

06 — Sectoral Plans: Four Focus Areas

Where the data becomes planning decisions

The Structure Plan addresses ten sectors. Documented below are the four where spatial analysis, GIS output, and field evidence most directly shaped the plan's recommendations.

06-A · Spatial Growth & Population

Managing 71% population growth without consuming agricultural land

Lalmai's population is projected to grow from 215,011 in 2022 to 366,964 by 2047 — a 71% increase in 25 years. The spatial growth strategy responds to this directly: densification of existing built areas first, regeneration of underperforming land second, strategic extension only into UDPI-designated suitable zones, and hard conservation exclusions around agricultural land, waterbodies, and the Lalmai Hills. Key finding: Only 30.76% of Lalmai's land area is suitable or better for urban expansion. The remaining 56.30% sits in conservation zones — mostly active double/triple-crop agricultural land that feeds the district. Growth management, not growth permission, is the plan's central spatial message.

06-B · Water Resources & Environment

A river system under industrial stress — and a hill ecosystem being lost

Water resources

Lalmai contains 3,172.93 acres of waterbodies — rivers, canals, beels, and ponds — that simultaneously provide irrigation, fisheries livelihood, and ecological habitat. The Dakatia River, the upazila's primary watercourse, faces a documented industrial pollution crisis: untreated effluent from the Cumilla EPZ is discharged directly into tributaries feeding the Dakatia, degrading water quality across the river system and threatening the livelihoods of thousands of farming and fishing households downstream. The Water Resource Plan recommends hard industrial discharge setbacks, blue-green buffer corridors, and restoration of natural drainage patterns disrupted by unplanned road construction.

Lalmai Hills ecosystem

The Lalmai Hill Range — the only elevated landform in Bangladesh's otherwise flat central plains — represents a critically important ecological singularity within the upazila. Field surveys documented a rich but threatened biodiversity: 47 tree species, 31 shrub/herb species, 23 bird species, 12 mammal species, and 14 fish species, including IUCN Red-listed and near-threatened taxa. Uncontrolled quarrying, encroachment, and deforestation are the primary pressures. The plan recommends strict conservation overlay on the Hills, aligned with the AHP exclusion already built into the UDPI model.

06-C · Disaster Risk Management

Waterlogging, floods, and seismic risk — baked into the zoning from the start

Disaster risk is not a separate chapter in this plan — it is embedded in the spatial decisions. The UDPI model already penalises low-elevation, flood-prone, and river-proximate zones. The Disaster Risk Management Plan formalises that logic into policy. Key risks identified:

Waterlogging Primary hazard — low-lying central and southern unions flood annually, rendering agricultural land unproductive for 2–3 months and damaging semi-permanent housing stock.
Seismic Exposure Lalmai sits in Bangladesh Seismic Zone II. Geotechnical classification places the majority of the upazila in SC/SD soil categories — soft soils that amplify ground motion, making building code enforcement in new development zones critical.
Lightning & Road Accidents Lightning strikes are the leading cause of non-flood fatalities. Road accidents are concentrated on R140 and R141 regional highways, which carry inter-district traffic through settlement areas with no grade separation.

The plan recommends hazard overlay mapping be embedded in all future development approvals, Nature-based Solutions (NbS) for drainage retention, seismic-resilient building typologies for new urban zones, and early-warning infrastructure prioritized in the Action Area Plans.

06-D · Transport & Connectivity

A road-only upazila with an underused rail line and zero functional waterways

Transport in Lalmai is almost entirely road-based — CNG auto-rickshaws, motorcycles, and local buses on a network anchored by the R140 Dhaka–Chittagong regional highway and the R141 Cumilla–Chandpur corridor. The dual-gauge Dhaka–Chittagong railway passes through the upazila but Alishwar Station is functionally dormant — an asset the plan identifies as a strategic opportunity for regional connectivity if revived. Despite 3,172+ acres of navigable waterbodies, no organized waterway transport operates, representing a lost last-mile and freight potential.

The Transport Plan prioritises: road surfacing and hierarchy rationalization within unions, a pedestrian and NMT (non-motorized transport) network connecting growth nodes, Alishwar Station activation as part of a regional transit strategy, and exploratory inland waterway transport feasibility for agricultural freight.
